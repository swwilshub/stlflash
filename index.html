What is Flashing?

The BMF S130 uses Projection Micro Stereolithography (PμSL) — a UV projector cures liquid resin one layer at a time. The very first layers are printed directly onto the build platform so the part stays anchored during the print.

At this interface, UV light scatters and reflects off the platform surface, curing resin slightly beyond the intended boundary of the part. This creates a thin rim of excess cured material around the base known as "flash" or "flashing".

Flash is typically 0.1 – 0.5 mm wide and only a few layers thick. It can usually be removed with light sanding or a blade, but it means the base surface won't have the same clean edge as the rest of the part.
Tip: Orient your part so the base face is a non-critical surface — avoid putting precision edges, sealing faces, or cosmetic surfaces on the platform side.

What is Squish?

To ensure reliable adhesion to the build platform, the first several layers of the print are slightly over-compressed against the platform. The projector also applies longer exposure times to these initial layers to ensure they bond.

This means the part can be ~1 – 5% shorter in the build direction (the axis perpendicular to the platform). The effect is most pronounced on shorter parts where the compressed layers make up a larger proportion of the total height.

The red overlay shows how your part's shape changes compared to the original (shown as the transparent grey ghost).
Tip: If a dimension is critical, orient the part so that axis runs parallel to the platform — not perpendicular to it. Dimensions in the platform plane (X/Z) are unaffected by squish.
